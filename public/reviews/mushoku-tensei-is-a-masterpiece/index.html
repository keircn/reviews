Foreword#

Whenever you see discourse regarding Mushoku Tensei online, it’s usually due to people making fun of its more controversial aspects. I’m not going to attempt to tell you how you’re wrong about Roxy being a loli because she’s actually a 50 year old demon. She is a 50 year old demon but she’s also certainly a loli. I think you can make fun of these parts of the novel (I also do it) and also appreciate the genuinely amazing storytelling.

I am only going to be referencing the light novel here, I have not read the webnovel and I am not familiar enough with the anime to comfortably review it in written form. I was originally planning to structure it arc-by-arc, but that would get incredibly long so I’ve decided to arbitrarily assign an era to each block of story arcs which occur in a similar time frame or share similar themes. With that said, I’ll get started.

Introduction#

The essence of Mushoku Tensei is that it is a story about growth, yet it also is not. Without getting into spoiler territory too early, it’s hard to put into words. The novel is written from the perspective of Rudeus Greyrat, but the world doesn’t exist for him. It is greater than him, far greater. This is a concept known as diegesis, or diegetic independence. Events occur without regard for the protagonist’s awareness and the timeline extends before and after than him. In the grand scheme of things, he’s just another person in this world.

The story begins much before he’s born into the Six-Faced World and it will not conclude until many, many years after his death. This simple fact is definitely not unique to Mushoku Tensei but I do think it contributes to how we view him. He has to earn everything he comes to achieve. However much talent he is born with, and he is born with profound talent unachievable to most humans, it isn’t talent alone that gets him to where he ends up.

He spent many months teaching Sylphiette magic, how to read, how to learn for herself. He spent years getting the stubborn, utterly stupid Eris Boreas Greyrat to be able to express herself and find common ground with others. He never gave up on training, no matter how powerful he became.

One of his greatest strengths is that he is constantly learning, it shows as you notice that he very rarely makes the same mistake twice — and when he does, it’s usually for plot convenience. What I’m trying to say is that Rudeus carves his own path through the world, it’s not paved for him. This is something that I will likely reinforce time and time again due to just how important it is to the story. The Six-Faced World may not be centered around him, but his story is.

The book you are reading is about the adventures of Rudeus Greyrat in this vast world.

Prologue#

A piece of trash. A good for nothing, unemployed, uneducated sack of human garbage. That is our protagonist. After being caught masturbating to loli on his computer while his parents’ funeral is proceeding, he is beaten, kicked out of his lair and left to rot on the street. He’s dirty, has nothing but the clothes on his back and no employable skills. Hopeless, our protagonist stumbles and fumbles his way along the sidewalk.

After contemplating his options, he sees a couple of high schoolers arguing in the middle of the road. Odd. He stands there for a moment, envious of them for being so carefree, before noticing a truck bounding towards them at high speed. Desperate to save them, he attempts to run towards them. His legs failing him, he tries to yell. No luck, he hasn’t used his voice in a long time. He ends up accomplishing nothing aside from yanking one of the kids out of the way.

He, along with two of the others, are hit by the truck at full force. The life slowly drains out of him and he dies.

Establishing The World#

Something that the author does incredibly well is actually during the very first time we even see _Rudeus Greyrat. I think Mushoku Tensei has one of the best first introductions to the “different world” in all of isekai. For example, the introduction to Re:Zero focuses primarily on a mechanical narrative hook rather than focusing on the world itself initially. That has nothing to do with the depth of the story, it’s just a difference in how the world is introduced to us.

The first thing we see once Rudeus gains consciousness in his new home is… Zenith’s breasts. Okay, I’m messing around. Seriously though, even in the first scene we experience, the author does something really clever and unusual for the genre. We cannot understand Paul and Zenith. It seems small, but it’s incredibly engaging. What sense does it make for a parallel world to share the same language? None! The fact that it doesn’t in this case serves to further convince you that this is a truly fantastical world.

During the lore dump in the prologue, something seemingly insignificant is repeated a lot: Rudeus (in his previous world) is exceedingly intelligent. He doesn’t quite demonstrate it in his later years as he pursues nothing and instead chooses to waste his talent on meaningless hobbies, but he is truly a bright young man during his school days. This simple fact is the catalyst that allows him to pick up the language of his new world in a matter of months, as well as learn to read at a high level of proficiency. It also allows him to experiment with things such as shortening incantations — or omitting them entirely.

It is at this point Rudeus learns of his monstrous mana capacity. Now, this is actually quite an interesting way to handle the trope of the mc of an isekai being overpowered. He isn’t just given a “cheat skill” by a cute goddess, there is a logical reason as to why he’s abnormally strong and it’s reproducible by others — Sylphie for example, just to a lesser extent. His immense power comes from his dilligence in honing his abilities from a very early age. The first time I saw this it was really cool to me because it does indeed make him incredibly overpowered, but it’s not unique to him. This is something others are capable of if they have the knowledge.

After a slight oopsie causing the wall of the upper floor of the house to… fall apart, we meet Roxy Migurdia — a blue-haired, 37 year old demon of the Migurd tribe with the appearance of a middle schooler. Roxy is actually a very important character. She doesn’t do much for most of his early and young adult years, but she has a huge impact early on. Roxy goes on to teach him all about history and magic and boring stuff like that. Then comes the day of his final exam. This is a huge milestone in his life thus far. The exam itself is unimportant, it’s that Roxy shows him the world outside isn’t so bad. She forces him to step out of his comfort zone and see for himself what people are like. His irrational fear of people disliking him evaporates, just like that — for now at least.

After the exam, Roxy concludes that there is nothing left for her to teach him and she names him a Saint-class water magician. Then, she leaves for the Shirone kingdom to take Pax Shirone as her pupil. This is the last we’ll see of her for a long time. Rudeus has gained a great deal of confidence from the time he spent with Roxy, he spends most of his time outside in order to practice his magic far away from his mother’s beloved trees.

One day, he encounters a group of bullies picking on a cute elf boy. Now, this is a really good point to take a moment and appreciate what is happening here. Rudeus, who was relentlessly bullied in his past life, has been rescued from his fear by Roxy. Using his newfound freedom and strength, he saves someone from being driven to the same fear he was. What a beautiful example of “Paying it Forward” so to speak. Not only did Roxy’s influence allow him to save Sylphie — both due to her helping him leave his home, and also giving him the literal strength necessary — but it also lead to Rudeus becoming Sylphiette’s mentor, in a sort of cyclical mentorship. This is just such an amazing series of events to me that I can’t help but praise it.

At this point, Rudeus and Sylphie have become good friends. Rudeus asks if his parents can pay for the two of them to go to the magic academy together, which they obviously decline. However, unbeknownst to Rudeus, his father strikes a deal with his cousin Phillip to have Rudeus tutor Phillip’s daughter Eris in exchange for the money he needs. And so, Rudy gets kidnapped by Ghislaine and sent off to the Citadel of Roa.
